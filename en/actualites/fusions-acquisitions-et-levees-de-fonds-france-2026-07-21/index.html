In focus — the deals we decoded

Mubadala acquires Pierre & Vacances: one billion for what, exactly?

Source: boursorama.com →

Mubadala Capital finalizes the acquisition of Pierre & Vacances-Center Parcs for approximately €1 billion through a public cash offer. Commitments to tender shares reached 80.13% of the capital — threshold crossed, operation locked. The group generates nearly €2 billion in annual revenue and owns the Maeva, Adagio, and Center Parcs brands.

The immediate interpretation: an Emirati sovereign wealth fund takes over a struggling French tourism asset, following a strategic review initiated in mid-2025. Industrial rescue, geographical diversification for Mubadala.

What's less obvious: Mubadala isn't buying a tourism operator; it's buying a European-style leisure accommodation infrastructure — villages, residences, parks — backed by real estate in structurally solid demand basins. The value isn't in the operating margins of a tour operator (thin, cyclical); it's in the stock of real estate assets and the ability to capture rent from the leisure time of European middle classes. For sovereign capital that manages in decades, not quarters, paying €1 billion for this infrastructure with a control premium is a landowner's logic, not a fund manager's. Concretely, for a French investor: this is a sign that tangible accommodation assets — undervalued since Covid — are becoming attractive again for patient capital with no five-year exit pressure.

Exitis absorbs Salamandre: the silent consolidation of fire risk

Source: lejournaldesentreprises.com →

Exitis, a consulting firm specializing in fire risk prevention and management (approximately 220 sites in France, headquartered near Tours), acquires Salamandre, a firm based in Draguignan with a presence in Marseille. Amount: €2 million. The operation strengthens Exitis's presence in Provence-Alpes-Côte d'Azur, particularly in the Var and Marseille area, and expands its offering to include fire safety system coordination and project management assistance.

A niche, clean, and clear build-up: fire risk is a regulatory market, therefore recurrent and non-cyclical. Exitis is methodically building national coverage through regional acquisitions — the logic is that of a local network whose value grows with density. Nothing spectacular, but exactly the type of consolidation that produces solid multiples upon exit.

The Raise Summit acquired: AI devours its own forums

According to Maddyness, the Raise Summit — a flagship event in the French startup ecosystem — is being acquired in an AI context. Neither the acquirer nor the amount has been disclosed in available sources.

A sober remark: when innovation conferences change hands, it's often a signal that their audience has become a commercial asset in its own right — a captive base of decision-makers that someone deems worth owning rather than renting. The trend of consolidation in B2B media and events within the French tech ecosystem is real, even if this specific case lacks details to elaborate further.

Bpifrance's Growth fund changes leadership

Source: maddyness.com →

Also according to Maddyness, Bpifrance's Growth fund is undergoing a change in leadership. No names or amounts have been disclosed in available sources.

A governance signal to watch: Bpifrance's Growth fund is one of the most active instruments in French growth equity. A change in leadership can influence sectoral priorities, average ticket sizes, and deal selectivity — though we cannot say more at this stage.

Fundora opens access to Prometheus, Bezos's AI startup

Source: maddyness.com →

Fundora, a French platform for accessing private investments, offers the opportunity to invest in Prometheus, Jeff Bezos's AI startup. Amount and conditions not disclosed.

The mechanism is that of democratizing access to closed funding rounds: Fundora acts as an intermediary to allow non-institutional investors to participate in deals usually reserved for large funds. The interest for a French investor is real — but the question of entry valuation, liquidity, and the rights attached to these indirect shares warrants serious due diligence before any enthusiasm.

Odiot acquires Lapparra: luxury silversmithing closes ranks

Source: boursorama.com →

Odiot SA, a Parisian silversmithing house founded in 1690, enters exclusive negotiations to acquire Lapparra Orfèvre Argentier. Amount not disclosed.

In artisanal luxury, acquisitions between houses of this type are never about volume: they target savoir-faire, archives, private and institutional clientele, and sometimes hallmarks. Odiot consolidates a technical and symbolic heritage that no one else can reproduce. For an acquirer in the sector, the rarity of these assets — and their near inalienability to external players — makes them targets of patrimonial as much as commercial value.

Advantics joins ABB: silicon carbide enters heavy industry

Source: lyoncapitale.fr →

ABB, a Swiss electrification and automation group, acquires Advantics, an Ain-based company (Saint-Genis-Pouilly) specializing in silicon carbide-based power conversion solutions. Amount not disclosed, completion expected in Q4 2026.

Advantics' solutions are used in data centers, industrial microgrids, power generation, and electric vehicle infrastructure.

Silicon carbide is to electronic components what hardened steel was to mechanics: it allows more power to pass through with fewer thermal losses, in a smaller footprint. ABB is integrating it to strengthen its direct current portfolio — exactly what data centers and decarbonized electrical grids need. A discrete technological building block acquisition, but one that positions ABB in two of the most capital-intensive infrastructures of the decade. For a French deeptech SME in this field: this is the type of industrial exit — absorption by a global group that needs the technology, not the brand — that is prepared ten years in advance.

Euralis and Maïsadour: the giant Southwest cooperative takes shape

Source: lejournaldesentreprises.com →

The Autorité de la concurrence (French Competition Authority) authorizes, with conditions, the merger between Euralis and Maïsadour, two major agricultural cooperatives in the Southwest. Amount not applicable (cooperative merger).

The imposed conditions remain to be specified, but the operation creates a national-scale cooperative entity in the corn, duck, seed, and agri-food sectors. In an agricultural sector under pressure (input costs, price volatility, international competition), critical size is no longer an advantage — it's a condition for survival to negotiate with large retailers and access export markets. The Euralis-Maïsadour merger follows the same logic as the dairy or cereal consolidations of previous decades: mutualize to avoid disappearing.

Samsung Biologics acquires PolyPeptide: **€1.7 billion** to enter GLP-1s

Source: endpoints.news → · Sector Industrial Tech & Manufacturing — 📬 subscribe to the Industrial Tech & Manufacturing newsletter

Samsung Biologics announces the acquisition of PolyPeptide, a Swiss CDMO specializing in the manufacture of API peptides, for $1.8 billion (approximately €1.7 billion) in cash — a 40% premium over the April share price. PolyPeptide estimates it produces about one-third of all commercial peptides worldwide, with 196 active projects, including 37 related to metabolic diseases (GLP-1) and 68 commercial products already on the market. Its 2025 revenue: €389 million, up 15.6%.

The obvious interpretation: Samsung Bio, until now focused on biologics and antibody-drug conjugates, is buying its way into peptides — and thus into the supply chain for anti-obesity drugs (Ozempic, Wegovy, and their successors), the most explosive pharmaceutical market of the decade.

The turnaround is structural: PolyPeptide is not an R&D lab; it's a factory. And that's precisely what the entire GLP-1 industry lacks — not the molecules (several are in development), but industrial-scale manufacturing capacity. Eli Lilly and Novo Nordisk are investing billions in their own factories because the constraint is not innovation, it's production. Samsung Bio is therefore buying a position in a global bottleneck. The 40% premium is the price of a rare infrastructure in a sector with capacity shortages. For a French investor or pharmaceutical industrialist: specialized peptide CDMOs are undervalued strategic assets in Europe — this operation signals that.

Persistent Systems acquires Nagarro: **€1.3 billion** for European weight

Source: facebook.com →

Persistent Systems, an Indian IT services group, announces the acquisition of Nagarro, a German digital engineering firm, for €1.27 billion — its largest acquisition to date. The stated objective: to create a global AI engineering entity with a strengthened presence in Europe, North America, and international markets.

Nagarro is listed, present in over 30 countries, and recognized for its capabilities in enterprise transformation and software engineering. The operation follows a well-established logic in IT services: mid-sized Indian groups buy their way into the European market through acquisitions of local firms, thus circumventing the commercial and relational barrier that direct sales from Pune or Bangalore represent. What Persistent is buying is not so much Nagarro's technology as its client accounts in Germany and Central Europe — business relationships that take ten years to build and can be acquired in a quarter.

🔮 The next move · hypotheses, not facts

  • intive — The acquirer seeks to strengthen its AI software engineering expertise and European presence after Nagarro.
  • PROFI Engineering Systems — Their expertise in digital transformation and cloud in Germany would consolidate their post-Nagarro European presence and strengthen their capabilities.

Elevion acquires BTS Biogas: Italian biomass changes hands

Source: euwid-recycling.com →

Elevion Group, a Dutch energy group backed by the Czech CZE group (partially public), acquires BTS Biogas, an Italian builder of biogas plants, from BTS Bioenergy Europe LLC (American). The deal includes a portfolio of nine biomethane plants in northern Italy (three in operation, six under conversion). Targeted combined capacity: approximately 30 million cubic meters of biomethane per year. Indicative amount: €80 million.

Italy has set a target of 5.7 billion cubic meters of biomethane by 2030 in its national energy-climate plan. Elevion, which aims for 70 million cubic meters of annual capacity by that deadline, is acquiring both a builder (EPC know-how) and a portfolio of operating assets — two distinct assets in a single deal. For a renewable energy investor: Italian agricultural biomass benefits from long-term incentives and a well-structured Snam grid-injected gas market — it's one of the most predictable renewable segments in terms of revenue.

Contents acquires Balio: sixth building block of an enterprise AI edifice

Source: eu-startups.com →

Contents, a Milanese platform for orchestrating AI agents for large enterprises, acquires Balio, a Barcelona-based company specializing in employee financial well-being in companies. Amount not disclosed. This is Contents' sixth acquisition in its European build-up strategy.

The logic stated by founder Massimiliano Squillace is clear: acquire enterprise software with established customer bases, then accelerate them via Contents' AI infrastructure. This model — buying the customer relationship, providing the technology — is a contemporary version of the software roll-up, with AI as a margin lever rather than simple cost mutualization. Its solidity depends on the ability to genuinely integrate acquired products without diluting the trust of existing customers — the sixth deal is also the first where integration becomes the real test.

Observit acquires Nordic InSupport: Nordic video surveillance consolidates into SaaS

Source: tradevenue.se → · Sector Media & Entertainment — 📬 subscribe to the Media & Entertainment newsletter

Observit (listed First North, Stockholm), a specialist in advanced video surveillance and AI data analysis for vehicles, acquires Nordic InSupport Network Video, a Swedish company expert in complex network video solutions, for SEK 66.7 million (approximately €6 million), paid in cash, new shares, and a vendor note, with a conditional earn-out of SEK 10 million linked to 2027 ARR.

InSupport reported 2025 revenue of SEK 49.2 million and EBITDA of SEK 5.9 million, with software ARR of approximately SEK 10 million growing strongly. Observit itself shows annual ARR growth exceeding 25%.

The pricing structure — part in shares, part in vendor note, an earn-out on ARR — is that of an acquirer who aligns the seller's interests with the transition to the recurring model rather than paying cash for a promise. This is sound capital discipline for an acquisition of this size.

Bain Capital acquires SupplyOn: European industrial supply chain enters private equity

Source: just-auto.com → · Sector B2B Software & Cloud — 📬 subscribe to the B2B Software & Cloud newsletter

Bain Capital announces the acquisition of SupplyOn, a supply chain collaboration platform for the automotive, aerospace, and defense industries, from its founding shareholders Aumovio, Bosch, Schaeffler, and ZF. Amount not disclosed. The platform connects over 200 major manufacturers and Tier 1 suppliers with over 140,000 suppliers worldwide.

SupplyOn is a textbook case of the transition Bain Capital wants to finance: an industrial B2B platform founded in 2000, built by and for corporates (Bosch, Schaeffler, ZF), reaching maturity precisely when its customers most need deep digitalization — supply chain disruptions, ESG compliance, the rise of defense. The industrial founders are selling because they are not in the software business; Bain is entering because the platform has a near-monopolistic position in its ecosystem and adding AI capabilities can multiply value without rebuilding the customer base. Concretely, for a French industrial subcontractor or principal: SupplyOn will likely accelerate its digital compliance requirements in the next 24 months.

Zenith Energy enters Italian biogas

Source: tipranks.com → · Sector Climate & Energy Tech — 📬 subscribe to the Climate & Energy Tech newsletter

Zenith Energy acquires 100% of an Italian biogas development company with a full permit and a construction-ready biomethane plant project, capable of producing approximately 3 million cubic meters of methane per year, injected into the Snam grid. Projected revenues at full capacity: approximately €5 million, EBITDA at €2.5 million (50% margin), supported by 15-year government incentives and a long-term supply contract with a regional authority. Financing planned through non-dilutive green debt.

A smaller deal than Elevion/BTS Biogas but the same market, same logic: Italy as a biomethane consolidation platform. The financing structure — green debt backed by revenues guaranteed by state incentives — is the model that makes these assets attractive to small-scale operators: revenue risk is almost zero over fifteen years; the risk is in project execution.

DataExpert acquires ADO Security: European cybersecurity integrates vertically

Source: thepaypers.com → · Sector Cybersecurity — 📬 subscribe to the Cybersecurity newsletter

DataExpert Group (Netherlands) acquires ADO Security (Copenhagen), a specialist in managed detection and response (MDR) and offensive security services (penetration testing, red teaming). Amount not disclosed. The operation combines DataExpert's digital forensics, threat intelligence, and regulatory compliance capabilities with ADO's offensive and continuous monitoring capabilities.

In cybersecurity, the real value of an acquisition like this is not in the tools — they change every eighteen months — but in the ability to offer a client end-to-end coverage: we test your defenses (offensive), we monitor continuously (MDR), we respond to incidents (DFIR), we help you comply (GRC). This complete cycle is what large organizations seek, and what only integrated players can deliver. DataExpert is building this model through successive acquisitions.

In focus — the deals we decoded

Proxima Fusion raises **€411 million**: nuclear fusion becomes a European industrial asset

Proxima Fusion, a German deeptech startup specializing in stellarator nuclear fusion, closes the largest European deeptech round in history with €411 million. The title of TFN's analysis is telling: "the deal wasn't about Google" — implying that the driver of this fundraising is German institutional architecture as much as private investors.

Nuclear fusion has long been the realm of perpetual promise — "available in thirty years, and always has been." What's changing: computing and simulation costs have plummeted, allowing for the optimization of complex magnetic configurations (the stellarator, unlike the tokamak, is intrinsically stable but difficult to design — it's exactly the type of problem that simulation AI solves). Proxima Fusion is the purest example of what the grid of technological thresholds allows us to read: a technology blocked for sixty years by the limit of available computing, suddenly unleashed by a leap in simulation power. The capital flowing in is not betting on physics — it's betting on the fact that the obstacle was not physics but computing, and that this obstacle has just fallen. For a French institutional investor: the absence of a French equivalent on this scale in fusion is a strategic fact, not an anecdote.

CuspAI raises **€414 million**: AI-driven materials discovery becomes sovereign infrastructure

Source: marketscreener.com → · Sector The Physical World — 📬 subscribe to the The Physical World newsletter

CuspAI (Cambridge, UK) raises $450 million (approximately €414 million) in Series B, valued at $2.6 billion, led by Kleiner Perkins and NEA, with participation from the British government and Jeff Bezos's fund. The startup simultaneously launches the AI Materials Foundry, a coalition of over 45 companies including Nvidia and Meta, to pool computing resources needed for the discovery of new materials — particularly for semiconductors.

Materials discovery is the invisible bottleneck of all technological transitions: batteries, semiconductors, superconductors, chemical catalysts — everything depends on materials whose discovery currently takes decades by experimental methods. CuspAI claims to compress this timeframe through large-scale simulation. The participation of the British government is not symbolic: it signals that sovereignty over materials discovery is now treated as a matter of national security, just like semiconductors. And the coalition with Nvidia and Meta to share compute is exactly the model of shared infrastructure — not a company selling a service, but a consortium building a common tool that each member needs. For a European industrialist or deeptech investor: CuspAI represents the type of ten-year bet that Europe has systematically underinvested in.

Syntetica raises **€26 million**: nylon recycling scales to industrial level

Source: recyclage-recuperation.fr →

Syntetica, a French deeptech specializing in the recycling of complex textiles (nylon and composite synthetic materials), closes a Series A round of $30 million (approximately €26 million), led by the Ecotechnologies 2 fund managed by Bpifrance as part of France 2030, with Swen Capital Partners, Lululemon, MAS Holdings, and EQT Ventures.

The presence of Lululemon as an industrial investor is a strong signal: a brand that buys large quantities of technical nylon is investing in its own recycled supply chain — this is both a technological validation signal and a guarantee of market access. For French capital in materials deeptech: Syntetica illustrates the hybrid financing model (public France 2030 fund + industrial clients + VCs) that allows crossing the valley of death between laboratory and pilot plant — the stage where most French deeptechs stop due to lack of patient capital.

Fleek raises **€23 million**: B2B second-hand seeks its infrastructure

Source: fr.fashionnetwork.com →

Fleek, a B2B second-hand fashion platform, raises $25 million (approximately €23 million). Investors not specified in available sources. The platform acts as an intermediary between professional sellers of second-hand clothing and reseller buyers.

The second-hand market is growing structurally, but the B2B segment — less visible than C2C (Vinted, Depop) — is where margins are potentially stronger because transaction volumes are larger and the commercial relationship is recurring. The real question for Fleek is not the market size (it's established) but the ability to build a robust enough logistics and trust infrastructure so that reseller buyers prefer the platform to their existing informal channels — this is the challenge for any B2B marketplace in a historically fragmented sector.

Sereact raises **€107 million** with Zalando: robotic warehouse automation accelerates

Source: tech.eu → · Sector Horizontal & Productivity SaaS — 📬 subscribe to the Horizontal & Productivity SaaS newsletter

Sereact (Stuttgart) raises $116 million (approximately €107 million) in Series B, with strategic participation from Zalando alongside financial investors. Sereact develops AI-driven robotic automation systems for warehouses.

Zalando's participation is not insignificant: the e-commerce group is one of the largest warehouse operators in Europe, and its equity investment in a robotics supplier is a signal of assumed strategic dependence. Zalando is not making a financial investment — it is securing priority access to technology it needs to maintain its logistics costs as volumes grow and labor becomes scarce. For a logistics operator or a fund investing in automation: when the main customer invests in the capital, commercial validation is acquired — the residual risk is technical execution and scalability.

Ulisses closes **€1 million** seed round: asset tracking in complex environments

Source: finsmes.com → · Sector B2B Software & Cloud — 📬 subscribe to the B2B Software & Cloud newsletter

Ulisses (Milan), a developer of IoT asset tracking and smart mobility systems, closes a seed round of €1.09 million, supported by the ARGO accelerator (co-funded by CDP Venture Capital and the Italian Ministry of Tourism), the national MOST center for sustainable mobility, and a syndicate of over 30 business angels.

The company started with mooring management in Italian tourist ports and has expanded to autonomous railway monitoring and warehouse logistics, via a patented low-power radio architecture in Europe (2.4 GHz). A seed round of this size funds the transition from R&D to initial commercial deployments — the critical phase where technology proves itself outside the lab. The diversity of applications (ports, rail, warehouses) is a strength for resilience and potentially a weakness for focus: the coming months will tell if Ulisses chooses a vertical or tries to address everything at once.
